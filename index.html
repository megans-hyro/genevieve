Skills reference

Every call type Genevieve handles

These are the calls your agents take hundreds of times a day. Tap any one to see example phrases and what to listen for.

Handles every call that ends with a human. Fires when a caller asks for a person, but also triggers automatically for urgent symptoms, identity failures, comprehension issues, and silence timeouts. Non-English callers transfer to the language line immediately. The key difference from a traditional IVR: Genevieve passes along what was already discussed, so the agent isn't starting from zero.

Try saying

→"I want to speak to someone." — with no reason given first, to see how she handles the re-ask.
→"I want to talk to someone about my bill." — should route straight to billing with no additional questions.
→"I've been feeling dizzy, can I talk to a person?" — should go to clinical screening, not offer a skill.
→"I'm having chest pain." — immediate transfer, no questions asked.
→Mid-triage: "Just connect me to someone." — listen for the nurse line transfer with the safety message attached.

⚠ Things to listen for

!Emergency instructions should be spoken as "nine one one" — three separate digits, not "nine hundred and eleven."
!You should never hear the routing destination read aloud (e.g. "transferring to front desk, non-clinical"). That's a bug.

Books new appointments across four visit types: General Wellness (30 min), Office Visit (30 min — requires 3 intake questions), Telehealth (30 min, same-day offered first), and Lab (15 min). New patients without an existing record get a registration link and care team callback rather than a direct confirmation.

Try saying

→"I'd like to schedule my annual physical." — wellness visit path.
→"I've had a sore throat for a few days, I need to come in." — office visit; expect three intake questions before any times are offered.
→During the office visit intake, give a non-physical reason — Genevieve should pivot and offer a telehealth visit instead.
→"I need to schedule my bloodwork." — books directly as an existing patient; transfers to central scheduling as a new patient.

⚠ Things to listen for

!Genevieve should always lead with a specific date and two times — never open with "what date works for you?"
!No weekend dates, ever. No mention of scheduling policy or minimum lead times in conversation.
!New patients should hear a registration link + callback confirmation, not "I've booked you for..."

Handles changes to appointments already on record. Each test persona has a next-business-day appointment. Cancellations now always prompt a reschedule offer before finalizing — this was added after a live test caught the agent moving straight to "anything else?" without offering to rebook.

Try saying

→"Can you confirm my appointment tomorrow?" — should read back the time and visit type on file.
→"I need to cancel my appointment." — listen for the reschedule offer before it finalizes.
→"Cancel it, and no, don't reschedule." — the reschedule question should be skipped since you answered it in the same breath.

⚠ Things to listen for

!A cancellation should never finalize without the reschedule offer, unless you already answered it unprompted.

How to test

→Dispute it: "I already had that done." — she should acknowledge the discrepancy and suggest confirming with the provider's office. No arguing.

⚠ Things to listen for

!James and Maria calls should never mention a care gap — there isn't one in their records.

Covers the full range of non-clinical patient questions: portal access, records requests, what to bring, referrals, insurance, and after-hours care. Hard stops are built in for anything that requires a licensed professional — lab results, medication guidance, clinical opinions. These cannot be unlocked by rephrasing the question.

Try saying

→"How do I access the patient portal?"
→"What do I need to bring to my appointment?"
→"Do you accept my insurance?"
→"Can you tell me my lab results?" — hard stop, every time, regardless of how it's phrased.

⚠ Things to listen for

!A clinical question phrased as small talk is still a clinical question. The hard stop should apply regardless of framing.
